Paideia

/pī-ˈdā-ə/
85% MASTERED

The holistic education of the ideal citizen in classical Greek culture. It encompasses gymnastics, grammar, rhetoric, music, mathematics, and philosophy to create a harmonious development of the soul and body.

Etymology

Ancient Greek παίδεια, from παῖς (paîs, "child"). The process of rearing toward excellence.

Cross-references

Socratic Dialogue Arete
002

Episteme

/i-ˈpi-stə-mē/
42% MASTERED

Justified true belief; the philosophical term for knowledge as opposed to mere opinion (doxa). In Foucault's work, it refers to the historical a priori that grounds knowledge and its discourses.

Etymology

Greek ἐπιστήμη, from ἐπίσταμαι (epístamai, "to know, understand").

Cross-references

Michel Foucault Doxa
003

Heuristic

/hyu̇-ˈris-tik/
98% MASTERED

Any approach to problem solving or self-discovery that employs a practical method that is not guaranteed to be optimal, perfect, or rational, but is instead sufficient for reaching an immediate goal.

Etymology

Greek εὑρίσκω (heurískō, "I find, discover").

Logos

/ˈlō-ˌgōs/
12% MASTERED

A term in Western philosophy, psychology, rhetoric, and religion derived from a Greek word meaning "ground", "plea", "opinion", "expectation", "word", "speech", "account", "reason", "proportion", and "discourse".

Etymology

Greek λόγος (lógos), meaning "reasoned discourse".
