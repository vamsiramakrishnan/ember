Teleology

/ˌtiːliˈɒlədʒi/

The explanation of phenomena in terms of the purpose they serve rather than of the cause by which they arise. In the Scriptorium, it refers to the intrinsic aim of a student's inquiry.

"The study of design in nature." — Struck 14 Oct.

Revised: Teleology is the pull of the future upon the present.

"If the seed contains the tree, does the tree cause the seed to exist?"

*See also: Aristotle, Metaphysics Book Θ

Eudaimonia

/juːdeɪˈmoʊniə/

Often translated as 'happiness' or 'flourishing', but more accurately described as the state of having a good indwelling spirit. It is the result of a life lived in accordance with virtue (*arete*).

"A feeling of deep contentment." — Deleted 02 Nov. Incorrect definition.

Note: My previous attempts at this were too focused on transient joy. Eudaimonia is a structural property of a life, not a momentary spike in dopamine.

Hermeneutics

/ˌhɜːrməˈnjuːtɪks/

The theory and methodology of interpretation, especially the interpretation of biblical texts, wisdom literature, and philosophical tracts. It involves the bridge between the past's intent and the present's understanding.

"Is it possible to understand an author better than they understood themselves?"
